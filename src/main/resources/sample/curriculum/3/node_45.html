Why do plates move? To understand plate movement, you need to learn about the players behind plate movement—the inner structure of the Earth.


If you could travel to the center of the Earth, what would your trip be like? As you start to travel beneath the surface, the rock around you would be cool. Then, at about 20 meters (66 feet) down, the temperature starts to rise. By the time you reach the core, the temperature is super HOT, between 6000 and 7000 degrees Celsius (~11000-13000 degrees Fahrenheit)! The pressure also increases as you travel deeper into the earth.


The three main layers of the Earth are the crust, the mantle, and the core.

  • The outermost layer of the Earth is the crust. The crust is a solid layer that includes both dry land and the ocean floor.
  • About 40 kilometers (25 miles) down, you cross the boundary into the mantle. The mantle is made up of very hot rock that is mostly solid.
  • The uppermost layer of the mantle and the crust together form a rigid layer called the lithosphere. This is the layer that contains the tectonic plates.
  • The layer of mantle below the lithosphere is called the asthenosphere. It is hotter and under more pressure, so it can flow very slowly, sort of like road tar softened by the sun.
  • The last layer of the mantle is the lower mantle, extending all the way to the core.
  • The outer core is a layer of molten (melted) metal, mainly iron and nickel. The inner core is solid iron and nickel.
